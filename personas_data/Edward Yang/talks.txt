## Spotlight on Edward Yang with Dennis Lim

URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BnY7vp5YBlE

I’m Dennis Lim.
I’m a film programmer and critic and also the artistic director
of the New York Film Festival. I think of Edward Yang as one of the most important filmmakers
of the late 20th century. Yang was absolutely
recognized in his lifetime. He showed at film festivals
and was a critic’s favorite, but I think,
at the time of his death in 2007, only one of his films,
Yi Yi, his final film, had received US distribution. He’s been recognized
by a lot of younger cinephiles, and I think that part of the reason
for his recent popularity is that these films have been
out of circulation for so long and so little seen, relatively speaking. But I think what’s really at the heart of Yang’s continued
popularity and relevance is the fact that he’s a filmmaker
who really understood his moment. He is a filmmaker for complex times. The first Yang film I saw
was A Confucian Confusion, and I remember being really struck
by how much was going on in this film. It had elements of almost screwball farce. The film seemed to understand what
certain tendencies in Taiwanese society were doing
to the people who existed there, who operated within this
hyper-charged atmosphere of the nineties as there was this large influx of money, and it was a moment
where the interpersonal was increasingly blurred
with the financial. Yang was a leading figure of what came to be known as the New Taiwanese Cinema
or the Taiwanese New Wave, and this is a movement
that emerged in the early eighties when Yang had just
moved back to Taiwan from the United States
where he studied and worked. And he was
alongside Hou Hsiao-hsien, who is his exact contemporary,
they were both born in 1947, and actually worked together, Hou Hsiao-hsien has a starring role
in Yang’s second feature, Taipei Story, and was also a co-writer of it,
and between the two of them, they made some of the most important films
of the 20th century. This movement coincided with this transformative period
in Taiwanese society. Taiwan had been under martial law
since 1949, it lasted nearly 40 years, and the eighties were a period of
this progressive liberalization for Taiwan and of this new wave of young filmmakers trying to understand
the complex situation of their country. Taiwan had a history of being occupied
by a number of countries: Japan, China, the US. The eighties is Taiwan’s passage
from authoritarian rule to a democracy. And the nineties is
another period of transformation just in terms
of the spread of global capital, something that he dealt with
in several of his films. And I think the world that
you see in Yang’s films is in some ways the world
that has come into being, it’s the world we exist in. And there’s a clarity
with which he is observing how these changes
affect human relationships. I also think of him as one of the great filmmakers of urban life and urban space. For the most part, his films are set
in Taipei, the city where he grew up. And like cities, Yang’s films are dense. They are sometimes sprawling. They are built on these interconnections and they’re full of life. Yang’s style, to a large degree, is a cinematic language
that was popular at the time. You could say descended from neorealism and inflected with European modernism, this kind of
meditative cinematic language that relies more on wide shots, that’s somewhat durational
and observational, that doesn’t use close-ups as much, that actually asks you
to step back a little bit from the action instead of immersing you in it. I’ve always thought that this
cinematic language of distance in Yang is key to what his interests are. It’s often thought of as
a language that is somewhat cold, distant, removed from the action. But I think that in Yang’s hands, more than in any other filmmaker,
this kind of language actually speaks to his curiosity and a certain kind
of analytical intelligence. I think, basically, for him, to understand people requires stepping back and observing them
in the systems in which they exist. I think he was great
at situating his characters in particular environments,
in the environments that shaped them, whether it was a city
or a family, a workplace. I think this sort of analytical side
of Edward Yang, I think you can trace it to perhaps
his background in the sciences. And there are certain elements of
how he frames and composes his films that you can trace
to his interest in comics and graphic novels and manga. But if you look closely at Yang’s films, I think there is quite a bit of variation
in rhythm and in mood. His first three films,
I think you see a filmmaker actually kind of playing
with cinematic language and form. And he makes, with his fourth film, this really mature, stately epic. A Brighter Summer Day was
the film that made his reputation. I remember it as a film
that was talked about more than seen in the 1990s. And then he pivots and makes
these two films in the nineties, A Confucian Confusion and Mahjong, which have a little bit more of, you could say,
genre stylization in them. Confucian Confusion is essentially
a comic question of whether Confucianism and capitalism are compatible as belief systems. And Mahjong has elements of
kind of a gangster thriller. And then he goes back to, I think,
the mode of A Brighter Summer Day to make– Of course we didn’t know it would be
his final film at the time, Yi Yi,
which contains all of human emotion. If I were to recommend an entry point
to the work of Edward Yang, I would say start with Yi Yi. It’s maybe unusual
to say start at the end, but there is something that feels like
a culmination in that film. It’s a film that includes
a birth and a death and a wedding and a funeral and it’s multi-generational. It is in some ways an attempt to see how much of human feeling
you can put into a film. And that’s something interesting
about Yang, too. It’s a relatively small
and compact body of work, but I’m often amazed
at just how much it contains, how much ground he covered
in terms of characterization, but also just how much historical
and social context is present in his films. So Yang had interesting collaborators
from the start. His first feature, That Day, on the Beach, stars Sylvia Chang,
who’s one of the biggest stars in Taiwan, certainly at the time. And it was shot
by Christopher Doyle, a cinematographer who would
go on to be really well-known for his work with Wong Kar Wai. A Brighter Summer Day stars
a very young Chang Chen, who was discovered by Edward Yang, and I think still is
one of the biggest stars in East Asia, known for his collaborations with
Wong Kar Wai and other filmmakers. I actually have a hard time pointing
to a personal favorite of Yang’s films, simply because I think I value
each of the films for different reasons, and that’s especially clear
as you return to them. I will say that I think the nineties’ films
are underappreciated, Confucian Confusion and Mahjong. And maybe because it was
the second Yang film that I encountered, A Brighter Summer Day, I think, is one of the great films
about adolescence and also Yang’s probably
most autobiographical film, a film that is inspired
by this actual murder case that was very shocking at the time, but that is part of the film,
but Yang really turns it into this really fully imagined and inhabited
portrait of a time, and you feel like it’s a film
that’s put together from sense memories of somebody
who lived through this moment. I think the reason I keep coming back
to Yang as a touchstone filmmaker is because he’s somebody
who is able to see through the murk of what was happening. It’s quite hard, I think,
to grasp the present, especially when things are changing
faster than we can apprehend. But I think he understood
how larger forces were reshaping our lives and our relationships with one another. And I think a moment like ours could badly use a filmmaker like that.


## Why Yi Yi Will Change How You See Life

URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjqjV0dVMPs

This is Edward Yang's last film from 2000, Yi Yi. It shows us the struggle of humanity in the modern age.  It became an essential piece 
of the Taiwanese New Wave And won countless awards, including 
Best Director at the Cannes Film Festival.  It's a culmination of Yang's earlier work, where   he explores the connection between people, the 
modern metropolis of Taipei and the sense of self. The aptly named film, which can be translated 
to "One One" or "One After Another" doesn't   Just show us how separated and alone
we are in the 21st century, But also the duality in ourselves. The characters find themselves buried under
the stress and monotony of daily life,   often unable to see themselves clearly. In 'Yi Yi,' Yang captures the entire spectrum 
of human existence—from birth and childhood,   through adolescence, adulthood, and old age, leading to the unavoidable reality of death. The sickness of the grandma, who is the matriarch 
in many Asian cultures, forces them to look at   their lives and themselves as individuals, 
disrupting their daily routines and robotic lifestyles.  When the doctor suggests they speak to the 
comatose grandmother, each family member   must face a mirror reflecting their own lives. Min Min, the daughter of the family matriarch, realizes that she has nothing to 
share. Her life feels meaningless,   her career is not making her happy, and only 
now can she see clearly that she is stuck.  Ting Ting has a special bond with her grandmother; 
she talks to her the most out of everyone in the   house, but her openness is partly due to guilt; 
Ting Ting forgot to take out the garbage,   leading to her grandmother's stroke. 
Ting Ting observes her neighbors;   she is curious but passive. She is open and 
kind, but in the end, this makes her vulnerable. NJ, the father of the family, 
is a successful businessman,   but he is also just going through the 
motions. The life in the metropolis,   however entangled he is in it, is not satisfying 
to him. He always looks deep in thought,   he is looking for a way out, or perhaps even 
just a few minutes where he can escape this   chaotic world, but life is pulling him 
back, and everything stays the same. NJ seeks the echoes of the past with music 
and finds solace in reconnecting with nature,   returning to the untouched state 
of the world before modernization.   Losing nature and living in unnatural 
conditions make human life difficult;   we become distant from ourselves, 
melting into the modern machine. After encountering an old lover and reminiscing 
about their connection, he eventually realizes   that he has no regrets; if he had a second chance, 
he would live the same life. This is the point   where we can see the real NJ — the contrast 
between his true self and his present self.   Despite his parents and society's pressure, 
he fought back and escaped the way they set   out for him, and this is why he has no regrets, 
because he stayed true to what he believes in.  In contrast to all this, the character 
who represents the freedom, creativity,   and curiosity so many lose in the 
process of growing up is Yang Yang. The silent 8-year-old of the family, who is always 
examining the world around him with curiosity,   he isn't just analyzing people he 
also has a connection to objects.  At the beginning of the film, we can already see 
him as an outsider, even among his peers. A small   boy who is being bullied because of his size. 
But he is constantly fighting back against the   pressure others put on him. In one of the first 
scenes, we can see him staring at the wedding   decorators putting up dozens of pink, heart-shaped 
balloons. This seemingly unimportant object,   which we later find out he brought with himself, 
will serve as a catalyst for his story. At the   reception, he uses the balloon as retaliation 
against the girls who bully him. Later on,   he is scolded by a teacher when the Tall Girl 
reports him for playing with something they   thought was a condom. Yang Yang is stoic and 
unwavering, and the teacher who can't oppress   him and show his authority is picking on him for 
it. The balloon motif is completed when he takes   revenge once more, now on the Tall Girl, but 
misses and hits the teacher. This leads to one   of the most iconic scenes of the film, where 
Yang Yang's coming-of-age story takes a turn. Hiding in the movie screening room, he sits 
down and observes as the Tall Girl walks in.   As her skirt catches on the doorframe for a 
moment, Yang Yang's perception of her shifts   abruptly—he realizes, for the first time, 
a stirring of attraction. From the simple   act of filling a heart-shaped balloon 
with water, we reach a turning point:   where Yang Yang loses control, 
sensing a brewing storm of attraction. Despite his small posture and funny scenes,   he is the character who brings up the 
most profound questions in the film.   Through him, Edward Yang can 
show the enthusiasm and hope   humans have, the joy of learning, of 
exploration, and of experimentation. Yang Yang's journey is one that shows the contrast 
between one's perception and true self. With his   camera, he is looking for things others can't 
see. The back of people's necks, the mosquito   on the wall, he wants to learn and understand 
the world around him and share it with everyone. A good example of this is the brother of 
Min Min, A-Di. He tries to convey himself   as someone successful, but in truth, he is always 
in debt, and the sole purpose of his marriage was   due to an unplanned pregnancy. His two sides 
are in stark contrast; his life is a lie.  Yang Yang is not lonely despite the bullying, he 
thrives alone and finds happiness in solitude. The back of someone is not just a part 
we don't look for or take note of;   it's also something that the person themselves 
can't see. Therefore, the obvious, the other half   of the truth is always with us; we just don't look 
for it, don't notice it. We look at things from   one perspective and accept them the way they 
are; Yang Yang, on the other hand, doesn't. Yang Yang looks below the surface and in 
the end, he takes a plunge into the unknown,   not afraid to get hurt. Throughout Yi Yi the director uses mirrors, 
reflective surfaces, windows, CCTV and   digital camera footage to create additional 
levels of framing and visual representation.   With this, the characters become split 
into two beings, which supports the film's   thematic and philosophical implications.
In a scene, where NJ and his coworkers   drive through the city, we can see 
that they are enveloped by the urban   landscape. Immersed and interconnected with 
this world, living life too self-absorbed   and comfortable, and the emotional state of 
the characters, the anxiety and depression,   ultimately comes from them failing to escape 
narrow-mindedness and embrace new viewpoints.  Not only this, but we viewers take 
a very unique place in this world:   we become the omniscient observer. The 
camera can look through the windows of   tall multistory buildings; we can peek 
into apartments, cafés, we are the voyeur,   the neighbor, and the city. The viewer 
gets to see the other half of the truth. The inner workings of the characters, their 
private romantic dates, their secrets and lies,   their struggle and despair, and 
the intimate details of a marriage. Just as Yang's camera stops 
and observes, we should too,   and just observe the world to show us its beauty. In "Yi Yi," Edward Yang not only portrays 
the human experience but also reminds us   of our greatest sin: losing curiosity 
about the world, ourselves, and each   other. We accept the world as it is presented, 
but Yang Yang tells us to stop and look beyond. Life is a timeless cycle of birth and death. 
People experience life's milestones—first love,   heartbreak, and the loss of family members. They 
try to make sense of the chaos through their   work and providing for their families, 
but ultimately we are not in control,   and the river of life just takes us and moves 
on. Despite how this sounds, it doesn't have to   be bad. We don't have to break the cycle to enjoy 
life. Edward Yang shows us we can turn the world   upside down and look at all this from a different 
angle.  We can notice the beauty in the world,
and find meaning in the things around us. We have to make the most of our lives,
teach ourselves, and teach others; only then can we see the other half of the truth.
